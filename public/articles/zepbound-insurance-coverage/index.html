Getting Zepbound (tirzepatide) covered by insurance is possible, but it’s often an uphill battle. Here’s the honest breakdown of what works, what doesn’t, and when it makes sense to just pay out of pocket.

Step 1: Check Your Actual Coverage

Before anything else, find out what your plan actually covers. Many people waste weeks on prior authorizations only to discover their plan explicitly excludes weight loss medications.

How to Check #

1
Call the number on your insurance card and ask: "Does my plan cover Zepbound for weight loss?" Get the answer in writing if possible.
2
Ask specifically about exclusions. Some plans exclude ALL weight loss medications. If that's the case, prior authorization won't help.
3
Check your formulary. This is the list of covered drugs. Zepbound may be Tier 3-5 (expensive) or not listed at all.
4
Ask about step therapy. Some plans require you to try (and fail) other medications first.

Step 2: The Prior Authorization Process

If your plan does cover Zepbound with prior authorization, here’s what that process looks like.

What Is Prior Authorization? #

Prior authorization (PA) means your doctor has to prove to the insurance company that you medically need Zepbound before they’ll cover it. It’s paperwork, and it’s annoying.

What Your Doctor Needs to Submit #

Typical PA Requirements

  • BMI documentation ‚Äî Usually 30+ (or 27+ with comorbidities)
  • Documented weight loss attempts ‚Äî Diet, exercise, sometimes other medications
  • Comorbid conditions ‚Äî Sleep apnea, hypertension, prediabetes, PCOS, etc.
  • Letter of medical necessity ‚Äî Your doctor explains why you need this specific medication

The Catch: Finding a Doctor Who Will Do It #

This was my biggest frustration. Many doctors:

  • Don’t want to deal with PA paperwork (it’s time-consuming and often unpaid)
  • Aren’t familiar with GLP-1s for weight loss
  • Work in practices that don’t prioritize weight management

Options:

  • Ask your PCP directly if they’ll submit a prior auth
  • Find an obesity medicine specialist (they do this all the time)
  • Use a telehealth platform that handles PA (like Ro or Found)

What to Do If You're Denied

Got a denial letter? You have options.

1. Appeal the Decision #

You have the right to appeal. The process:

1
Request the specific reason for denial in writing
2
Have your doctor write a detailed appeal letter addressing their concerns
3
Include additional documentation (lab results, specialist letters, weight history)
4
Submit within the deadline (usually 60-180 days)

Reality check: Appeals take time and energy. Success rates vary wildly. If your plan has a blanket exclusion, appeals rarely work.

2. Ask About Mounjaro Instead #

Mounjaro is the same medication (tirzepatide) but approved for Type 2 diabetes instead of weight loss. If you have prediabetes or diabetes, your doctor might be able to prescribe Mounjaro instead ‚Äî and some insurance plans cover it more readily.

3. Skip the Fight, Go Self-Pay #

  • Finding doctors to write prior auths was a hassle
  • The mental energy wasn’t worth it

I got a prescription for Zepbound and went straight to self-pay through LillyDirect.

The Bottom Line #

Worth trying insurance if:

  • Your plan covers weight loss medications
  • You have documented comorbidities (especially sleep apnea)
  • You have a doctor willing to fight for prior auth
  • You have time and patience for the process

Skip straight to self-pay if:

  • Your plan explicitly excludes weight loss meds
  • You’ve already been denied and don’t want to appeal
  • You want medication now, not in 6-8 weeks
  • The hassle isn’t worth your mental energy
